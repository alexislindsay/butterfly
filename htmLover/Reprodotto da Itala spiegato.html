Systemic Assumptions Behind AI's Carbon Footprint

Carbon Footprint of AI Systems: Linking Environmental Cost to Structural Choices

The Carbon Footprint of Large AI Models

AI models have grown massive in recent years, and so have their energy demands. Training a state-of-the-art model often requires thousands of powerful GPUs or TPUs running for days or weeks. Researchers found that training a single large NLP model (with extensive neural architecture search) could emit over 626,000 pounds of CO₂ – equivalent to the lifetime emissions of several cars​projectexigence.eu. In fact, the carbon emissions from training some models are comparable to transcontinental flights​projectexigence.eu. These figures highlight that achieving slightly higher accuracy often comes at a steep environmental cost, leading scholars to question if the performance gains justify the energy impact​projectexigence.eu.

Importantly, it’s not just training that consumes energy – inference (using the model for predictions) also has a significant footprint. For instance, operating OpenAI’s ChatGPT at scale requires enormous electricity: running hundreds of millions of queries on ChatGPT was estimated to consume about 1 GWh of energy per day (roughly the daily usage of 33,000 U.S. households)​nakedcapitalism.com. According to one professor, answering a question via ChatGPT can be 10 to 100 times more power-hungry than doing a basic search or email query​nakedcapitalism.com. As AI adoption grows, these energy costs could skyrocket. Already in 2022, the surge of generative AI led to a notable spike in data center power and water usage​cepr.org. Analysts predict that, at current trends, data centers (many of which run AI workloads) could account for 8% of U.S. electricity demand by 2030 – up from about 3% in 2022​cepr.org. This paints a worrying picture: left unchecked, AI’s carbon footprint may expand exponentially, much like cryptocurrency mining did​nakedcapitalism.com.

Massive AI models rely on sprawling data-center infrastructure. With the rise of generative AI, companies like Google and Microsoft have reported sharp increases in data center energy and water usage​cepr.org. For example, Google’s data centers consumed 20% more water in 2022 compared to the prior year, while Microsoft’s jumped by 34%, largely due to AI-driven computing needs​cepr.org. Projections indicate that such facilities could draw 8% of U.S. power by 2030 (up from 3% in 2022) if AI growth continues unabated​cepr.org. These centralized server farms concentrate enormous computing power—and energy consumption—illustrating how AI’s carbon footprint is fundamentally tied to its industrial-scale infrastructure.

Scarcity and Artificial Friction in AI Development

Behind the technical aspects of AI lie deeper economic assumptions like scarcity and artificial friction. Modern AI development often treats compute power as a scarce (and therefore highly valuable) resource. Only a handful of organizations possess the “compute scale” needed to train frontier models like GPT-4, which in practice creates an artificial scarcity of advanced AI capabilities. By pouring vast sums into exclusive supercomputing clusters, these actors reinforce the notion that bigger is always better – even if it means shouldering enormous costs. This mindset has historical roots: for years, progress in AI (especially deep learning) has been achieved by using more data and larger models, assuming an endless race of scale. As a result, the field often chases state-of-the-art performance with brute-force methods instead of prioritizing efficiency, implicitly accepting scarcity of efficiency and abundance of compute spending.

“Artificial friction” comes into play when companies intentionally or unintentionally make AI less accessible or efficient than it could be. One example is the choice to keep trained models proprietary. Models like GPT-3 or GPT-4 were trained on huge public datasets, but their creators do not release the model weights publicly – they are accessible only via paid APIs. In effect, an abundant public resource (Internet text) is converted into a private, scarce product. This artificial scarcity is a design choice: nothing physical prevents widely sharing a model (copying digital weights is trivial), yet companies impose friction (through licensing, APIs, paywalls) that centralizes control. This strategy protects intellectual property and revenue streams, but it also means many researchers and smaller players face artificial barriers to using cutting-edge AI. The result is a concentration of AI benefits in those with resources, while others encounter friction at every turn (e.g. rate-limited APIs, high cloud compute costs, lack of open source code).

Another form of artificial friction is seen in how the AI discourse itself sometimes downplays resource concerns. Remarkably little public attention has been given to AI’s energy drain, leading some critics to suggest that it’s not in the interest of industry leaders to highlight these inefficiencies​nakedcapitalism.com. As one commentator observed, the relative silence on AI’s high energy cost “confirms the notion that AI concernists are really out to get AI regulated simply to create moats to protect tech titans”​nakedcapitalism.com. In other words, some stakeholders may prefer discussions about AI ethics and safety that result in complex regulations (which they can manage), rather than discussions that would force them to internalize environmental costs. Such selective focus can be seen as artificial friction at the policy level – it steers the conversation away from sustainability (which might demand fundamental changes or slow down AI deployment) and toward issues that potentially advantage incumbents. The outcome is that the true cost of AI (energy, carbon, etc.) remains less visible, allowing the status quo of heavy resource use to continue largely unchallenged.

Finally, the very architectures and code we use can embody artificial friction. Many AI models are not optimized for energy efficiency by default. Researchers often train models with dozens of trials, or use computationally heavy techniques (like exhaustive neural architecture search) that could be made far more efficient​projectexigence.eu. This happens in part because academic incentives reward beating benchmarks, not minimizing kilowatt-hours. The result is a culture of compute maximalism, where using 2× the compute to get a 1% improvement in accuracy is seen as acceptable. In essence, the lack of built-in incentives for efficiency is an inherited friction in the research process – a structural issue where the easiest path to success is to throw more resources at the problem. As we’ll see, these patterns aren’t inevitable; they stem from the broader economic and competitive system that AI is built on.

Exploitation in the AI Supply Chain

AI’s impressive capabilities often mask a hidden web of exploitation underlying the technology. This exploitation occurs at multiple levels: of labor, of communities, and of natural resources.

Labor Exploitation: Advanced AI models don’t spring forth fully formed – they often require human help to refine or filter their outputs. A stark example came to light when it was revealed that OpenAI used outsourced Kenyan workers paid less than $2 per hour to label toxic content and make ChatGPT safer​time.com. Dozens of workers had to read and tag horrific texts (including descriptions of abuse) to help the AI learn to avoid toxic language. This behind-the-scenes toil was done for extremely low wages, under psychologically harmful conditions, to benefit a Silicon Valley company soon valued at $29 billion​time.com. The situation has been described as “modern day slavery” by observers​qz.com. OpenAI is not alone – many AI datasets are cleaned or annotated by gig workers in developing countries, reflecting a broader pattern of global labor exploitation in AI development. Large firms benefit from cheap crowdwork (for data labeling, content moderation, etc.) while those doing the work see very little reward. This raises ethical concerns about fairness and whether the true social cost of AI is being accounted for. The deployment of AI also threatens to exploit labor in another sense: automation can displace workers, and without equitable policy responses, it can perpetuate inequality – though that is a broader socioeconomic effect beyond the immediate scope of AI model training.

Resource and Environmental Exploitation: The AI supply chain begins in mines and factories around the world. Training hardware – GPUs, TPUs, and vast server farms – rely on minerals like lithium, cobalt, nickel, gold, and rare earth elements. Extracting these comes at a human and ecological price. For example, lithium mining in the Atacama Desert of Chile (home to some of the world’s largest lithium reserves) has been labeled a form of tech colonialism​cepr.org. Mining companies divert massive amounts of water in this arid region to extract lithium for batteries, depriving local indigenous communities of water and damaging ecosystems​cepr.org. Locals have protested how these projects proceed with minimal environmental oversight, causing displacement and loss of livelihoods​cepr.org. This is just one case; across the globe, communities near cobalt mines in the Congo or rare earth processing sites in China similarly bear the toxic burdens of our high-tech economy. In these places, environmental exploitation and human health risks (pollution, hazardous working conditions) are the underside of AI’s shiny coin.

Even after the materials are mined and the chips manufactured, the exploitation continues in the form of energy and water use. Data centers supporting AI consume not only electricity (often from fossil fuels) but also cooling water. If located in regions with cheap coal power or weak regulations, AI training can effectively exploit lax environmental policies to cut costs. An analysis of cloud computing providers noted that some major data center regions rely heavily on coal and natural gas, greatly exacerbating carbon emissions​projectexigence.eu. In contrast, companies like Google and Microsoft have made efforts to purchase renewables for their data centers​projectexigence.eu. This means the carbon footprint of AI can vary dramatically based on whose infrastructure is used – the same model could be relatively green or extremely polluting depending on energy sources. When profitability is the main driver, there is a risk that companies will choose the cheaper energy (often fossil-based) unless pressured otherwise. In essence, they exploit the atmosphere by dumping carbon emissions into it — a classic tragedy-of-the-commons problem inherited from the broader economy.

Community Exploitation: The presence of enormous server facilities can strain local communities as well. Large data centers have been known to draw heavily on municipal power grids and water supplies. For instance, the spike in water usage by AI-heavy data centers means nearby towns might face shortages or ecological impacts. Local residents often have little say in these deployments, and the jobs created are relatively few once construction is over. E-waste is another externalized cost: after a few years, AI chips and servers become obsolete and are discarded. Much of this electronic waste is offshored to developing countries for recycling or disposal, where it can pollute communities that had no share in AI’s benefits​cepr.org. The world produced 62 million tons of e-waste in 2022 (a figure rising rapidly) and only a tiny fraction of critical materials (like rare earths) is recovered through recycling​cepr.org. The rest ends up in landfills or scrap yards, often in the Global South. Generative AI’s rapid development cycle – constantly pushing for new chips and hardware – is accelerating this e-waste problem​cepr.org. Thus, from cradle (mining) to grave (disposal), AI’s lifecycle often involves shifting burdens to those least empowered: underpaid workers, marginalized communities, and future generations who will inherit the environmental damage.

In summary, exploitation in AI is not a side issue but a structural feature: cheap labor, cheap resources, and externalized environmental costs have greased the wheels of AI advancement. These practices mirror longstanding patterns in the global economy (for instance, apparel or electronics industries have similar issues). The key difference is that AI’s impacts can be less visible to consumers – it’s easy to interact with a clever chatbot or a recommendation algorithm without seeing the human and natural exploitation that enabled it. Bringing these hidden costs to light is essential for a holistic understanding of AI’s true price.

Centralization of AI Power and Resources

AI’s development has become highly centralized, both in terms of who owns the needed resources and who reaps the benefits. Training cutting-edge AI models today requires a convergence of capital, expertise, and infrastructure that only a few tech giants possess. This has led researchers to dub these firms the “digital lords” of the AI age​cepr.org – a handful of companies that effectively control the compute farms and data required for modern AI.

Consider the requirements to train an GPT-class model: access to massive datasets (often scraped from the entire internet), thousands of expensive accelerators (GPUs/TPUs), and a reliable supply of electricity (potentially tens of megawatt-hours per training run). Only organizations like Google, OpenAI (backed by Microsoft), Meta, or government-funded supercomputing labs can marshal such resources. This concentration was highlighted when Microsoft built a dedicated supercomputer for OpenAI with tens of thousands of GPUs to facilitate training of GPT-3 and beyond​medium.com. Google, similarly, has hundreds of TPU pods (their proprietary AI chips) in its data centers available for internal AI projects.

The result of this resource concentration is that state-of-the-art AI research is no longer a level playing field. Academic researchers or small startups cannot easily compete with results coming from these “compute-rich” institutions. In 2019, a prominent study already warned that the financial and energy costs of large-scale NLP models were hindering equity in research, since only well-funded labs could afford to participate​aclanthology.org. This problem has only deepened since. We now have a compute divide: an informal barrier separating the few AI-rich organizations from the many AI-poor. Those with access to giant clusters can push the frontier forward (e.g. creating models with 100+ billion parameters), while those without must watch from the sidelines or collaborate on smaller-scale innovations.

Centralization also manifests in data. The best AI models benefit from proprietary or hard-to-gather datasets – think of Google’s trillion search queries a year (which it can use to refine AI), or Facebook’s social graph data. These troves are not shared publicly, giving big tech a data advantage in addition to compute. Even when the data is “public” (like web crawl data), only groups with huge storage and processing capability can truly exploit it. Thus, the knowledge and digital exhaust of billions of people are funneled into training models under the control of a few entities. This dynamic has raised concerns of monopolistic control: if a handful of companies end up providing all the AI services (much like only a few provide web search or cloud computing), they gain enormous economic and political power. AI centralization could reinforce existing Big Tech dominance, a phenomenon some have termed techno-feudalism in which users become dependent on AI services gated by corporate lords (hence “digital lords” analogy).

Another consequence of centralization is reduced diversity in AI development. When only a few players drive most progress, their values, goals, and blind spots can shape the technology. For example, OpenAI and Google primarily compete on who can get a more capable model to market; thus they both prioritize scale. Ethical or energy considerations might take a backseat unless they also align with competitive interests. Meanwhile, alternative approaches (like smaller, more interpretable models, or decentralized federated learning approaches) may be under-explored because they don’t fit the dominant narrative of central big models. The homogenization of AI research around the “bigger = better” paradigm is partly a function of who’s in charge: the ones who can go bigger do so, setting benchmarks that others feel compelled to follow.

It’s worth noting that centralization has a self-reinforcing quality. As AI models become products (e.g. cloud AI APIs, AI-powered services), the revenue from those products flows back to those same companies, allowing them to invest even more in compute and talent, widening the gap. The massive investment needed to train frontier models acts as a high barrier to entry for new competitors – which again creates an “artificial scarcity” of competition. In economic terms, we might be seeing the formation of an AI oligopoly or even quasi-utility. Society then relies on a few companies for AI, which raises questions about resilience (single points of failure), national security (if one country’s firms lead, others feel dependent), and public accountability.

However, we are also seeing early moves to counter centralization. There are open collaborations (for instance, the BigScience project that produced the BLOOM model with 176B parameters as a public, open-access model) aiming to democratize access to large models. And in 2023, Meta’s LLaMA model (65B parameters) was released to academic researchers and subsequently leaked to the public, leading to a proliferation of downstream projects by independent developers. These events suggest that while training is centralized, access need not be completely centralized. After LLaMA’s weights became widely available, developers proved they could run and fine-tune large language models on consumer-grade hardware by applying clever optimizations (quantization, distillation). In effect, they brought pieces of AI capability out of the data center and into laptops and smartphones. This trend offers a glimpse of a more decentralized future: one where many people can run moderately advanced AI locally, rather than having every AI task handled by a big tech cloud.

Still, the baseline remains: today’s most powerful AI systems are deeply reliant on central infrastructure and decision-makers. This centralization is not purely a technical necessity; it’s also a product of economic structures – venture capital funding, corporate strategies, and a winner-takes-all mindset – which we examine next.

Inefficiencies and Inherited Assumptions: Necessary or Not?

A critical question arises: are the energy inefficiencies, resource concentration, and inequities in AI byproducts of the technology itself, or are they the result of choices shaped by our broader economic system? There is growing evidence that many of these issues are not inevitable but rather stem from structural and systemic assumptions carried over into AI.

One key insight is that AI’s escalating energy use is largely a design choice – researchers and companies have often chosen to prioritize raw performance over efficiency. In fact, the paradigm in AI research has been: if a bigger model yields better results, use the bigger model. This mirrors a broader assumption in industry that more consumption is acceptable if it leads to more output (a mindset rooted in industrial-age growth models). However, studies suggest we could do things differently. For example, Schwartz et al. (2019) introduced the notion of “Green AI”, advocating for a research culture that values not only accuracy but also efficiency and carbon footprint​projectexigence.eu. They pointed out that many tasks could be solved with far fewer computations if we put our mind to it, and urged reporting of energy metrics for AI models​projectexigence.eu. The fact that such recommendations are even possible indicates that current inefficiencies are, to a large extent, unnecessary. They persist mostly because the incentives and assumptions in the field haven’t prioritized change.

Another perspective comes from looking at trends in compute vs. efficiency. Hardware is indeed improving – GPUs today deliver far more operations per watt than a decade ago. But AI workloads have been growing faster than those efficiency gains. An analysis from OpenAI noted that the compute used in the largest AI training runs was growing on an exponential curve (doubling every few months), vastly outpacing the roughly 10× efficiency gain per decade in hardware​medium.com. In other words, any natural gains from Moore’s Law or better chips have been eaten up and exceeded by the appetite for scale. This reflects a structural drive for growth over sustainability. It’s reminiscent of how increasing fuel efficiency in cars over past decades was offset by people driving more miles or buying more vehicles — a phenomenon economists call the Jevons paradox or rebound effect. In AI, whenever we get a bit more compute efficiency, we reinvest it into an even larger model. This cycle is not a law of nature; it’s a result of human choices (often guided by competition – each AI lab racing to outdo the others). Breaking out of this cycle might require shifting what we value (e.g. rewarding the AI community for achieving the same result with half the compute, rather than only praising the new state-of-the-art accuracy).

The assumption of scarcity plays a subtle role too. Modern economics is built on the idea of scarce resources – and historically, computing power was indeed a scarce resource. In the mid-20th century, only governments and big corporations had mainframes. That legacy lingers in how we approach AI: there’s an implicit belief that AI progress requires huge, scarce resources, so we accept centralization and high barriers as “natural”. But computing is not as scarce as it once was; we have unprecedented aggregate compute globally, just unevenly distributed. Some argue that if we network and coordinate better, we could tap into a more distributed abundance of computing (for example, folding@home style distributed computing for AI, or leveraging idle compute on millions of devices). The fact that we haven’t seriously attempted this for large-scale AI is an inheritance of the centralized mindset. Instead of questioning “Do we really lack enough compute overall, or is it a self-imposed scarcity due to how it’s allocated?”, the field mostly doubled down on centralization. So, the scarcity is in many ways artificial – a product of how access is structured, not absolute unavailability.

We should also consider economic exploitation as an inherited practice. The AI industry did not invent exploiting cheap labor or externalizing environmental costs – these are common in many industries (fast fashion, electronics, etc.). AI is simply following the path of least resistance within a capitalist framework that historically has undervalued environmental and social well-being in favor of growth and profit. The difference is that AI presents itself as a high-tech, futuristic endeavor, giving the impression of a clean, purely digital product. This can obscure the fact that it’s embedded in the old economy of mining, factories, and offshore labor. In that sense, AI’s unsustainable aspects are inherited from the broader systems of production and “lack”. By “systems of lack,” we refer to societal systems that create a sense of never having enough – never enough profit, never enough speed, never enough data – driving a continuous extraction and expansion. AI, operating within this paradigm, ends up reproducing the same patterns: always needing more data, more compute, more users, more investment, with insufficient regard to consequences like carbon emissions or worker fatigue.

Are these patterns necessary for AI to succeed? Many critics say no. For example, it’s possible to imagine an alternative route of AI development: one focused on smaller models, federated data, and frugal use of resources, progressing via clever algorithms rather than brute force. Some researchers are indeed exploring this, but it’s not the dominant approach because the current market and academic incentives don’t reward it as much. It’s not technically impossible to do AI in a decentralized, energy-aware way – it’s just harder to fund and glamorize. There are encouraging signs, though, that necessity (in the form of climate urgency) may mother some invention here. Groups are beginning to ask, “Who should own and control the key infrastructures that drive AI, especially under a climate emergency?”​cepr.org. This reflects a meta-level critique: if current owners are locked into unsustainable practices, perhaps shifting ownership or governance could change the outcomes. Furthermore, international bodies like UNESCO have started to assert that AI systems “should not be used if they have a disproportionate negative impact on the environment”​cepr.org, implying that environmental considerations should override pure performance in some cases. These are nascent ideas, but they represent a reframing of AI’s purpose: from one of endless pursuit of capability (underpinned by assumptions of lack and competition) to one of meeting human needs within planetary boundaries.

In summary, much of AI’s current environmental and social toll comes from structural design choices, not absolute necessities. The choices were influenced by broader systems – capitalism’s drive for growth, the tech sector’s winner-take-all dynamics, and a historical lack of accountability for environmental externalities. The encouraging flip side is that by altering our priorities and assumptions, we could design AI development to be more sustainable and equitable. The challenge is largely political and economic, not just technical.

Who Exemplifies These Assumptions, and Alternatives on the Horizon

To concretize this discussion, let’s look at some key players and models that embody the problematic assumptions – and contrast them with those pursuing different paths:

  • OpenAI (GPT Series) – Scale-First and Centralized: OpenAI’s GPT-3 and GPT-4 are iconic examples of the bigger is better ethos. GPT-3 was trained on hundreds of billions of words using thousands of GPUs and an extraordinary amount of electricity (exact figures aren’t public, but estimates run into hundreds of tons of CO₂ for training)​projectexigence.eu. OpenAI exemplifies centralization: it went from a non-profit to capped-profit, took a $10 billion investment from Microsoft, and now tightly controls its most advanced models behind an API. The company has also exploited cheap labeled labor (outsourcing moderation tasks to places like Kenya for <$2/hour) to fine-tune its models’ outputs​time.com. In many ways, OpenAI built an AI powerhouse by assuming that scaling up (data, parameters, compute) would yield intelligence – and it did yield very impressive results. But this came with energy inefficiency (GPT-4’s training likely required even more compute than GPT-3) and a concentration of AI capability (only those who pay or partner with OpenAI get access to the full model, an instance of artificial scarcity). OpenAI’s approach is being critiqued for its environmental impact and lack of transparency; for example, the company has not disclosed the carbon footprint of GPT-4, and this opacity itself is criticized in light of calls for AI energy accountability​nakedcapitalism.com.

  • Google (PaLM, LaMDA, etc.) – Tech Giant’s Burden: Google has developed numerous large models (such as PaLM with 540 billion parameters, and LaMDA, the basis of its Bard chatbot). As one of the “digital lords,” Google has the advantage of a vertically integrated stack – it designs its own chips (TPUs), operates its data centers, and has vast data resources. Google’s AI models are also kept internal or offered as services, not released openly (aside from research papers). Google tends to mitigate some environmental costs by purchasing renewable energy and optimizing data center efficiency. Indeed, Google was an early leader in carbon-neutral operations (through offsets) and aims for 24/7 carbon-free energy by 2030. Despite this, the absolute resource use for Google’s AI is enormous. The 20% spike in water usage in Google’s data centers in 2022​cepr.org was largely due to cooling needs from new AI workloads – showing that even an efficiency-focused company faces sustainability challenges when scaling AI. Google’s models also illustrate centralization: they are so computationally expensive that only Google’s infrastructure can run them well. This has sparked internal discussions at Google about whether chasing ever-larger models is worth the cost, or whether to focus more on efficiency and smaller models that can be deployed widely (a debate playing out after seeing OpenAI’s success with a very large model). In short, Google embodies both the centralization and an attempt at partial sustainability (through green energy), but it remains to be seen if that’s sufficient as AI demand grows. Notably, Google’s research has proposed faster, cheaper algorithms (like knowledge distillation, federated learning on edge devices, etc.), indicating they are aware of alternatives, even if the headline-grabbing achievements still come from giant centralized models.

  • NVIDIA – Arms Dealer of the AI Boom: NVIDIA isn’t an AI model creator, but its influence is enormous as the provider of the GPUs underlying most AI systems. NVIDIA’s business model exemplifies the centralization and scale paradigm: it sells high-end chips in bulk to cloud companies and large institutions. The more everyone believes in scaling up AI, the more GPUs NVIDIA sells – so the company has little incentive to encourage frugality. In fact, NVIDIA has been reporting record data center revenues (for example, about $701 million in one quarter of 2019, and much more in recent quarters) as AI usage soars​medium.com. This corresponds to tens of thousands of new GPUs being deployed worldwide​medium.com. Each of these GPUs can consume 300W to 500W under load, and often they are clustered by the hundreds – so you can imagine a single AI supercluster drawing 1–5 MW of power. NVIDIA does work on making each generation of GPU more performance-efficient, but as noted, the demand for compute has outpaced these gains​medium.com. The company has started promoting the idea of using AI to optimize power usage (for instance, using its chips for climate modeling or grid optimization) – a kind of techno-optimist counter-narrative to its own footprint. Importantly, NVIDIA’s dominance (nearly 80-90% share in AI accelerators) also means the hardware supply is a choke point: scarcity of GPUs became a real issue during various booms (crypto mining, then AI booms). This led to price inflation and effectively rationing of who could get GPUs. In this way, NVIDIA’s control created an artificial friction in AI development – not out of malice, but simply because when one company’s product is the gatekeeper, access is limited. Alternatives like Google’s TPUs (not sold, only in cloud) or AMD’s GPUs (far behind in AI software support) haven’t yet broken this centralization. However, seeing this risk, many governments (and firms like Tesla, Graphcore, Cerebras) are developing their own AI chips to decentralize the hardware ecosystem. NVIDIA exemplifies how a single actor can both enable the AI revolution and also constrain its shape through proprietary control and a focus on ever-higher performance.

  • Meta (LLaMA) – A Partial Shift to Openness: Meta (Facebook) took a slightly different approach recently by releasing LLaMA, a large language model, to the research community (albeit under a non-commercial license). Though the full model weights leaked, it turned into a surprising experiment in decentralized AI. Suddenly, thousands of independent developers had access to a model on par (in many tasks) with OpenAI’s older GPT-3, and they began fine-tuning and optimizing it for various devices. Within weeks, someone had managed to run a cut-down version of LLaMA on a phone, and others created GPU-efficient variants that can run on a single 8GB graphics card. This was a stark contrast to the typical closed AI paradigm. It demonstrated that when given the artifact (the trained model), a broad community can adapt and deploy AI in a much more distributed way. Meta still trained LLaMA in a centralized, resource-intensive way (using its internal infrastructure, likely incurring significant energy usage and cost), so the training side was not decentralized. But the distribution was. This points to a hybrid model: perhaps not every organization needs to separately train a 100B-parameter model (which would be wastefully redundant); instead, a few models could be trained and then shared openly, and the community could handle fine-tuning and deployment at the edges. That would greatly cut down on duplicate energy expenditures and also lower the barrier for entry, addressing some scarcity issues. Meta’s motivation wasn’t purely altruistic (they gained academic good will and a foothold in setting open standards), but it stands as an example of moving away from artificial scarcity. In contrast to OpenAI, Meta gave out (some of) the “recipe” freely, showing a different set of values – at least in that instance.

  • BigScience BLOOM – Collaborative and Transparent: The BigScience project, a year-long open research collaboration with over a thousand participants, culminated in the BLOOM language model (176B parameters). This effort, supported by the French government’s supercomputing center and Hugging Face, exemplifies a more equitable and transparent approach. Instead of a private company training a model in secret, BigScience shared regular updates, published their code and training logs, and even documented the energy consumed and carbon emissions for training BLOOM. Thanks to France’s largely nuclear energy grid, the team reported a relatively moderate carbon footprint for BLOOM’s training (on the order of tens of tons of CO₂, which is lower than it could have been on a coal-heavy grid). They also implemented novel governance for the model – including a responsible AI license and an inclusive approach to training data (sourcing from many languages and communities). While BLOOM still required a centralized supercomputer to train, the key difference was philosophical: the process was public-minded. Once trained, BLOOM was made openly available, complete with details on how it was built. This represents an alternative to the trade-secret style of model development. It shows that, structurally, we can choose to build AI as a public good. The limitation, of course, is funding – BigScience relied on government and institutional support (a form of public investment). But it sets a precedent that not all transformative AI models must be proprietary or built on exploitation. BLOOM’s existence provides a contrast to GPT-3: similar scale, but done with transparency, global collaboration, and an eye on impacts. It’s a hint that the assumptions of centralization and secrecy are not the only way to do cutting-edge AI.

  • Hugging Face and Open-Source Ecosystem – Democratizing AI: Hugging Face, a company and community platform, has spearheaded the open-source AI movement. By hosting thousands of pre-trained models and providing tools to share and reuse them, it lowered the entry barrier for doing AI. This ecosystem challenges the idea that AI knowledge should be siloed. Many models on Hugging Face are smaller and more efficient, aiming to solve real problems without giant compute. For example, there are distilled versions of BERT and other large models that run on normal laptops or phones. The success of this ecosystem (with millions of downloads of models) indicates a strong demand for decentralized AI usage – where developers can take a model and run it on their own device or infrastructure. Hugging Face has also advocated for measuring carbon footprints of models and even provides tags for “carbon emissions” on some model cards. This culture brings awareness and pushes back on the norm of ignoring environmental impact. Companies like EleutherAI (a grassroots research collective) also fit here – they produced open models like GPT-Neo and GPT-J by pooling volunteer and sponsor resources. Although EleutherAI had to rely on borrowed cloud credits (again highlighting that compute is centralized even if the project is community-driven), the fact that their models are openly available contrasts with the closed model approach. These actors exemplify a push towards decentralization and sustainability, at least in terms of access and reuse. They show that many researchers and practitioners are eager to move beyond the scarcity model – if given access to models and tools, they prefer to build on them collaboratively rather than reinvent the wheel in isolation.

  • Emerging Sustainable AI Efforts – Efficiency and Local AI: On another front, we see efforts to break the energy inefficiency assumption through research. DeepMind’s Chinchilla strategy, for instance, demonstrated that instead of increasing model size infinitely, one could get better results by using a balanced approach (smaller model, more training data), effectively doing more with the same compute budget. Such insights can reduce waste by avoiding drastically oversized models. There’s also growing interest in sparsity and model compression – techniques that allow big networks to be pruned or quantized so they run faster and use less power without much loss in accuracy. If broadly applied, these could cut down the energy usage of both training and inference. On the hardware side, startups are exploring low-power AI chips (neuromorphic computing, analog processors) that could perform AI computations with orders of magnitude less energy. If successful, that could fundamentally change the equation, decoupling AI capability from massive energy draw. Additionally, federated learning and edge AI represent architectural shifts: instead of centralizing data and computation in one place, they distribute the training across many devices (e.g., phones training a joint model without sending raw data to the cloud). This can alleviate the need for one giant data center doing all the work (though the total energy might or might not be lower, depending on the scenario). The key point is that these alternatives illustrate how AI can be re-thought to be less centralized and more efficient. They challenge the inevitability of the current paradigm by providing proof-of-concept that competitive AI is possible on smaller scales and with smarter resource use.

In contrasting these actors and models, a pattern emerges: the dominant players (big tech companies, leading AI labs) often embody the assumptions of scarcity, centralization, and tolerance for inefficiency/exploitation, whereas the alternatives – be it open collaborations, academic/community efforts, or new tech focusing on efficiency – seek to subvert those assumptions. We are at a juncture where the AI community is increasingly aware of these issues. The question is whether the meta-level critique (linking environmental cost to design choices) will translate into a meta-level shift in how we design and deploy AI. Will companies begin to compete on efficiency and sustainability and not just raw capability? Will governments impose rules that make energy transparency and low carbon footprint a requirement for AI systems (as the EU has started hinting at​nakedcapitalism.com​nakedcapitalism.com)? There is some momentum: the EU’s AI Act will require logging energy use​nakedcapitalism.com, and discussions about carbon taxes or incentives for green computing are starting​nakedcapitalism.com.

Ultimately, achieving a more sustainable and equitable AI ecosystem may involve re-imagining the structural design choices. This means valuing quality over quantity (efficient algorithms vs. brute force), openness over proprietary lock-in (to avoid redundant efforts and artificial scarcity), and long-term societal benefit over short-term race dynamics. The actors exemplifying change show it’s feasible: e.g., sharing a model openly can yield innovation without each player repeating the same costly training; prioritizing efficiency can still deliver breakthroughs (as seen when researchers dramatically reduced the size of models needed for translation and other tasks by clever methods).

In conclusion, the carbon footprint of AI systems is not just an engineering issue but a reflection of deeper systemic choices about how we build technology. Scarcity, exploitation, centralization, and friction are neither new nor unavoidable – they are historically rooted in how we’ve organized economies and innovation. AI has inherited these structures, but it doesn’t have to perpetuate them. By recognizing the links between AI’s environmental costs and its design assumptions, we can begin to pivot towards AI that is both powerful and sustainable. That means critically examining who builds AI, with what resources, and for whose benefit. If the community and policymakers demand it, AI can be developed under new assumptions: abundance of knowledge sharing, respect for ecological limits, decentralization of power, and removal of artificial barriers. Such a shift would ensure that the next generation of AI not only dazzles with its intelligence, but also aligns with the planet’s and society’s long-term well-being.
